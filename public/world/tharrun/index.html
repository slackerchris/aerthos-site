The dwarven script of Aerthos

The Tharrun

The sacred cuts — twenty runes of Tharrudin, the tongue of Tharros the Forgefather. Each rune a channel for the mountain's deep fires.

Language Tharrudin Seeded from Tharros, the Forgefather Cut, never inked 20 runes · 2 sacred marks · base-twelve reckoning

The Runes

Every rune's name is also a root word — learn the alphabet and you learn the lexicon. The anvil-mark Tharr comes first, and no inscription begins without it.

The Two Sacred Marks

Two of the runes are not letters at all, but the Forgefather's own tools. They bracket every true inscription: the anvil opens it, the hammer seals it.

The anvil holds, the hammer strikes, and making is the space cut between them. It is the theology of the forge-god carved into the punctuation of the script itself — nothing is thar, true-made, until struck. The hammer's holy singular is Dhomm, the Forgefather's own hammer, the relic the Common tongue calls the Forgehammer; its everyday form is the common noun pelvor.

How it's spoken

  1. Every rune is sounded. No silent letters — dwarves distrust ornament. What is cut is said.
  2. Pure, open vowels. Latin values: a=ah, e=eh, i=ee, o=oh, u=oo. A circumflex holds them long and reverent.
  3. The rolled R is the spine. Single r taps; rr trills at double length. Doubling it lengthens reverence as much as sound — sacred words carry it.
  4. TH softens to DH between vowels. θ (“thin”) at a word's start; voiced ð (“this”) when it falls between two vowels. The hammer-mark Dhomm keeps the soft ð — say “THOHM.”
  5. Stress the first written vowel. The first vowel in a word; the first root in a compound — VOR·rom, THAR·ru·din. A ghost breath never takes it.
  6. Clusters take a ghost vowel. A hard onset eases on an unwritten breath — Halvor → “HAL-vor,” dravok → “d'RA-vok.” The breath is never carved and never stressed.
  7. Stay low. Spoken level and deep. A rising tone is kept for oath and invocation — to lift the voice otherwise is, to a dwarf, to lie.

To speak it

Dur ath val.
stone · and · oath
The standard greeting — well-met; I stand with you. Answered: “Val hald.” — the oath holds.
Tharros gorn, Tharros ren.
Forgefather's fire · Forgefather's ring
The forge-blessing, spoken over a finished piece — the words cut into Bjorn's axe.
Pelvor ath Tharr.
hammer · and · anvil
A maker's oath, sworn over work begun — the two tools of the Forgefather, the strike and the thing that holds.
Valos thar.
the oath · made sacred
The sealing line. “This is the oath.”

Words with no Common tongue

tharren — the resonance a rightly-made thing holds. Its opposite, morren, the dead-ring of a thing unmade or defiled.

durfenn — the ache of the deep in one who is far from it. Homesickness for the mountain, felt as weight.

morgorn — the unmaking of the made; a forge run backward, fire that unmakes instead of makes.
